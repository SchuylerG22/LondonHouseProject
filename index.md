During the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, England was a nation suffering from an identity crisis. With internal conflict, continuously shifting national boundaries, and increasing multicultural interactions, England’s identity was constantly in flux. As its citizens sought desperately to cling to any sense of self, the desire for a concrete “national identity” began to form and subsequently spread throughout the country. With people constructing their ideal national characteristics, any behaviors in opposition to that paragon, such as perversity, violence, and, most importantly here, eating improperly, were ostracized. Thus, certain diets and consumption practices became distinguishably “English,” giving food an unprecedented political and social vitality.  

To further this consumption-centered identity, English dietary writers created common enemies by demoralizing the eating practices of other nations, particularly Ireland. However, there has been much suspicion amongst historians as to how different English and Irish consumption practices truly were. How could two nations with such similar agricultural landscapes and environmental compositions form such contrasting eating rituals? Did these portrayed differences in consumption have any truth in them, or were they entirely invented out of the emerging nationalistic and anti-Irish sentiments?

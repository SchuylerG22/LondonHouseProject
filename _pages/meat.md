Due to its mass popularity, beef was one of the foods most closely associated with Englishness and “proper” English eating habits. During this period, English dietaries constantly wrote about the benefits of beef to the English body, promoting it for mass consumption. For instance, the dietary writer Thomas Moffet’s work Health’s Improvement (1590-1604), states that beef is “most nourishing unto English bodies,” even going so far as to state, “there is not a better meat under the Sun for an English man.” Moffet also asserts a common contemporary opinion that there was a distinctive and superior nature to English meat compared to other cultures, stating, “Cowbiefe is supposed by the Irish people, and also by the Normans in France to be best of all: neither do they account so much of Oxen; either because they think 7 the unperfit creatures, or rather (as I take it) because they know not how to use and diet them in the gelding.”  With such writings, the dietary authors of this period created a character of the English person based on their connection to the consumption of beef. According to historian David Gentilcore, the English much preferred the aesthetic of “the meat-eating, plain-speaking Englishman versus the politic and wily Italian/ Frenchman/ Spaniard.” Therefore, the proper consumption of beef was a characteristic the English people happily took on, seeing the affront of such consumption as direct opposition to the English way of being.
